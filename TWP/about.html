Why Wacky Waste

I was inspired to start Wacky Waste because when I was walking around campus, I noticed so much trash, on the floor and filling the trash cans, and it made me wonder how much we truly waste everyday. Then this curiousity made me wonder how much trash is thrown out in a week, months, and lifetime, and why do we continously do this. Were the things we throw out thrown out for good reason or can we reuse it for something else.

This is why Wacky Waste was created. It was my way of digitally blogging my experience with trash and what I am throwing out each week. It's a way to highlight the liftspan of my "junk" and say a proper goodbye before I dispose of it.

Additional Inspirations

This project was also inspired by Sarah Newman and her views on Trash. Though we view trash as waste that needs to be discarded, a lot of the time it is not, and depending on the culture, it can still be worth a lot. She also goes in depth on how we think we are getting rid of trash, but in reality, it comes back to us into our bodies, “As inhabitants of industrialized cities, we often think of landfills as places set apart for things to decay, deteriorate and vanish, but in reality, landfills tend to offer ideal conditions for preservation”. A lot of the time, especially in a materialistic society that we live in, we buy so many things without realizing how much trash we dispose of because of it. This had me reflecting on the amount of waste I consume, and so I thought Wacky Waste would also help me be more environmentally conscious.

Another article that supported my project idea was Viz Muniz's who is an artist that shared his personal experience of the connection he had with the National Museum of Brazil. He uses his sadness and channels it into inspiration for art. He used the resources of the fire rubble into his art and gave the profit of his art back to the museum. This story really touched me and demonstrated the power of recycling and turning angry and sadness into something beautiful. I thought that I could channel my inner artist with this project and reuse my trash one last time into something meaningful.

Lastly, I was also heavily influenced by the article Waste Reimagined, which highlights Detroit artists like Halima Cassells and Hannah Tizedes who collect trash from rivers and shorelines and transform it into powerful artwork to raise awareness about plastic pollution and climate change. Their work builds on Detroit’s history of trash-based art, showing how creative reuse can inspire emotion and environmental action more effectively than just stating facts.
